
# Data Card: ARPX Synthetic Benchmark v2

Datasheet following [Gebru et al. 2018](https://arxiv.org/abs/1803.09010).

---

## Motivation

### Why was this dataset created?

To enable methodological ML research on physiological risk
stratification for pharmacologically enhanced athletes — a population
for whom (a) real labelled data is not available due to legal,
ethical, and stigma-related barriers; and (b) general-population
clinical reference ranges systematically misclassify normal-for-cohort
biomarker values as pathological.

### What gap does it fill?

No prior public benchmark exists for this population. Athlete-monitoring
ML datasets focus on injury risk and overtraining, not enhancement-aware
risk classification.

### Who created it?

Vishal Hota (KL University Hyderabad) under self-supervised research
context. No external funding.

---

## Composition

### What does an instance represent?

A single cross-sectional snapshot of an enhanced athlete: demographics,
training behavior, hormonal state, hepatic/renal/cardiovascular/
hematological biomarkers, behavioral self-reports, and contextual
metadata.

### How many instances?

| Split        | n    | %    | Class distribution            |
|--------------|------|------|-------------------------------|
| Train        | 1800 | 60%  | low 53%, moderate 39%, high 8%|
| Validation   | 450  | 15%  | preserved via stratification  |
| Calibration  | 300  | 10%  | preserved via stratification  |
| Test         | 450  | 15%  | preserved via stratification  |
| **Total**    | 3000 | 100% |                               |

Splits are deterministic (seed=42) and reproducible.

### What is each feature?

19 features in total:

**Demographics (4)**
- `age` (int, 18–60): years
- `sex` (categorical: male/female)
- `weight_kg` (float, 50–140): body mass
- `bf_percent` (float, 5–35): body fat percentage

**Training & recovery (3)**
- `training_vol_hr_wk` (float, 4–20): weekly training hours
- `sleep_h` (float, 4–10): nightly sleep duration
- `goal` (categorical: bulk / cut / recomp / maintenance)

**Endocrine (3)**
- `testosterone_total` (float, 200–1800 ng/dL)
- `estradiol` (float, 8–120 pg/mL)
- `enhancement_load` (float, 0.4–1.7): composite scalar

**Hepatic (2)**
- `ALT` (float, 8–80 U/L): alanine aminotransferase
- `AST` (float, 10–60 U/L): aspartate aminotransferase

**Renal (1)**
- `creatinine` (float, 0.6–1.8 mg/dL)

**Cardiovascular (2)**
- `HDL` (float, 25–80 mg/dL)
- `LDL` (float, 60–220 mg/dL)

**Hematological (1)**
- `hematocrit` (float, 35–58 %)

**Behavioral self-reports (2)**
- `mood_score` (int, 1–10)
- `libido_score` (int, 1–10)

**Contextual (1)**
- `status` (categorical: off / on / cruise / pct)

### What is the label?

`risk` ∈ {low, moderate, high}, generated by a deterministic scoring
function applied to the biomarkers. Specifically:
- Score points awarded for threshold crossings on hematocrit, ALT,
  AST, HDL, LDL, and the composite enhancement load.
- Cumulative score thresholds map to the 3 classes.

The exact scoring rule is documented in the paper's Appendix A but
withheld from public release as a misuse mitigation
(see "Considerations" below).

### Are there missing values?

No. The synthetic generator produces complete records.

### Confidentiality

All instances are synthetic. No real patient or athlete data is
present.

## Collection process

### How was the data acquired?

The data was synthesized — not collected. The generator is a Python
script (`src/data_generator.py`) that:

1. Samples each feature within published physiological ranges for
   enhanced athletes
2. Applies cross-system correlations documented in clinical literature:
   - Testosterone ↔ hematocrit (Coviello et al. 2008)
   - Oral compounds ↔ ALT/AST elevation (Pomara et al. 2015)
   - Endocrine shift ↔ HDL suppression / LDL elevation (Hartgens 2004)
3. Clamps values to clinical bounds (no negative creatinine, etc.)
4. Applies the deterministic scoring function to assign risk labels

### Reference sources

Bounds and correlations sourced from:
- Bhasin et al. 2010 (Endocrine Society testosterone guideline)
- Stout / Ohlander 2018 (polycythemia and TRT)
- Grundy et al. 2018 (AHA cholesterol guideline)
- Levey et al. 2009 (CKD-EPI eGFR equation)
- Prati et al. 2002 (ALT reference intervals)
- Kicman 2008 (anabolic steroid pharmacology)
- NHANES 2017–2018 (general-population marginals)

### External validation

Marginal distributions for ALT, HDL, LDL, hematocrit, and creatinine
were compared against NHANES 2017–2018 (N=6,738 for HDL). Kolmogorov-
Smirnov distances were below 0.08 for all five markers in the
non-enhanced subpopulation, indicating distributional shape preservation.

The active-enhancement subpopulation deliberately departs from
NHANES distributions (testosterone shifted upward, hematocrit
shifted upward, HDL shifted downward) consistent with documented
PED effects.

## Preprocessing

For modeling, categorical features (`sex`, `status`, `goal`) are
one-hot encoded into 11 binary columns, yielding 27 model input
features. The encoder is fitted on training data only and reused
across splits to prevent leakage.

SMOTE oversampling is applied to the training split only (high-risk
class oversampled to balance with low-risk). Validation, calibration,
and test splits retain natural class distributions.

## Uses

### Recommended uses

- ML research on uncertainty quantification in clinical tabular data
- Benchmark for conformal prediction and counterfactual explanation
  methods
- Educational reference for synthetic clinical data construction

### NOT recommended

- **Predicting real-world clinical outcomes.** The labels are scoring
  rule outputs, not clinical adjudications.
- **Drawing population-level inferences about athletes.** The data
  is synthetic.
- **Training models for deployment without clinical revalidation.**

## Limitations and considerations

### Demographic gaps

- Approximately 85% male (matches published surveys of PED
  prevalence; Sagoe et al. 2014) — but underrepresents female
  enhanced athletes
- Reference ranges sourced from US/European literature; populations
  with documented biomarker variation (South Asian creatinine,
  CETP-mediated HDL variation) are not separately modeled

### Label circularity

The labels are functions of the features. A sufficiently expressive
classifier (e.g., a depth-8 decision tree) can recover the scoring
rule and achieve 99.97% accuracy without learning any clinical
relationship. Any model performance number on this benchmark must
be interpreted as scoring-rule approximation accuracy, not clinical
prediction accuracy.

### Cross-sectional only

Each instance is a snapshot. Real clinical risk often manifests as
biomarker trajectories over time. Longitudinal extension is planned
but not present in v2.

### Misuse mitigations

The exact scoring function is withheld from public release. The
license prohibits enforcement and insurance use. The deployed demo
includes a honest-disclosure footer.
